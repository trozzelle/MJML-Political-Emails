                 When Frederick Douglass took the stage at the Corinthian Hall in Rochester, NY in 1852, he did it not to tear down our founders- he said so himself- but to wonder how a nation built on independence could so willingly hinge its prosperity on violently enslaving others. As Douglass spoke, millions still toiled in bondage for which our country has never made real amends.

Douglass declared, "I am not included within your pale of glorious anniversary...the rich inheritance of justice, liberty, prosperity and independence, bequeathed by your fathers, is shared by you, not by me." July 4th means something different for many of our neighbors. It’s not a celebration of independence, but a reminder of how our country has always allowed for a few to prosper at the expense of the many.

How many are asking, still, 150 years after we abolished slavery from our nation, for basic equalities that many enjoy and celebrate? Echoing from the past, louder, is Douglass' answer: "We need a stir, an earthquake, a whirlwind."

Did Douglass know his words on a July 4th more than 150 years ago would still be so important today? I think so.

History doesn't happen for us, or happen to us. We make our history. We made our history in a hot, sticky, flickering candle-lit room in 1776. Fredrick Douglass made history again in Rochester in 1852, and he told Americans to keep making it for the better, generations later. He's still whispering to us now.: don't settle, try harder. Fight. Don't stop. Keep marching. Keep voting. Make history.

As we isolate at home, separated by a pandemic from family and friends, essential workers toil to get our nation's "rich inheritance" in the streets while the 1% gets even wealthier. We need to remember that history isn't always great, but we can do our founders proud as we look back and remember- but only so long as we look forward and continue the fight for equality.

Wishing you and your family a safe and healthy holiday weekend to remember.



  In solidary
  Nate McMurray
